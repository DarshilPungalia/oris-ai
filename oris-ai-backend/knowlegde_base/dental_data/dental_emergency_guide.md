# Oris AI Dental Emergency Guide

## Dental Emergencies Classification

### URGENT - Seek Immediate Care (Within 1 Hour)

#### Severe Dental Pain
**Symptoms:**
- Throbbing, intense pain
- Pain that prevents sleep
- Pain radiating to ear, jaw, or neck
- Swelling of face or jaw
- Fever accompanying dental pain

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Rinse with warm salt water
2. Take prescribed or over-the-counter pain medication
3. Apply cold compress to outside of face (20 minutes on, 20 off)
4. Avoid hot or cold foods/drinks
5. Contact emergency dental service immediately

**Do NOT:**
- Place aspirin directly on tooth or gums
- Apply heat to the affected area
- Ignore signs of infection (fever, swelling)

#### Knocked-Out Tooth (Dental Avulsion)
**Time Sensitive - Success depends on quick action**

**Immediate Steps:**
1. Find the tooth and pick it up by the crown (white part)
2. Do NOT touch the root
3. If dirty, rinse gently with water for 10 seconds maximum
4. Try to reinsert the tooth immediately:
   - Hold tooth by crown
   - Place in socket
   - Bite down gently on clean cloth

**If Cannot Reinsert:**
- Place tooth in milk (best option)
- Alternatives: Patient's saliva, saline solution
- Do NOT store in water
- Do NOT let tooth dry out

**Get to dentist within 30 minutes for best outcome**

#### Dental Trauma with Bleeding
**Symptoms:**
- Profuse bleeding from mouth
- Visible broken or displaced teeth
- Jaw pain or inability to close mouth normally
- Facial swelling or bruising

**Immediate Care:**
1. Control bleeding with clean gauze (bite down firmly)
2. Apply cold compress to reduce swelling
3. Save any tooth fragments
4. Seek immediate emergency care
5. If jaw injury suspected, go to hospital emergency room

#### Severe Infection/Abscess
**Warning Signs:**
- Severe facial swelling
- Difficulty swallowing
- Difficulty breathing
- High fever (over 101Â°F)
- Swollen lymph nodes

**Immediate Actions:**
1. Contact emergency dental service
2. If breathing difficulty, call 911
3. Take prescribed antibiotics if available
4. Use cold compress for swelling
5. Stay hydrated

### SEMI-URGENT - Seek Care Within 24 Hours

#### Broken or Chipped Tooth
**Assessment:**
- Large chip with pain: More urgent
- Small chip without pain: Less urgent
- Sharp edges cutting tongue/cheek: Needs attention

**Immediate Care:**
1. Save tooth pieces if possible
2. Rinse mouth with warm water
3. Cover sharp edges with dental wax or sugarless gum
4. Use cold compress if swelling
5. Take pain medication if needed

#### Lost Filling or Crown
**Symptoms:**
- Sensitivity to temperature
- Discomfort when chewing
- Visible hole in tooth

**Temporary Measures:**
1. Keep area clean
2. Use temporary dental cement (pharmacy)
3. Avoid chewing on affected side
4. For lost crown: Try to place back with denture adhesive
5. Schedule dental appointment within 24-48 hours

#### Moderate Dental Pain
**Characteristics:**
- Persistent but manageable pain
- Sensitivity to hot/cold
- Pain when chewing

**Management:**
1. Over-the-counter pain relievers
2. Warm salt water rinses
3. Avoid trigger foods/drinks
4. Keep area clean
5. Schedule appointment within 24 hours

### NON-URGENT - Schedule Appointment Within Week

#### Minor Tooth Sensitivity
**Symptoms:**
- Brief pain with hot/cold foods
- No constant pain
- No swelling

**Self-Care:**
1. Use desensitizing toothpaste
2. Avoid extreme temperatures
3. Gentle brushing with soft toothbrush
4. Schedule routine appointment

#### Small Food Particle Stuck
**Safe Removal Methods:**
1. Gentle flossing
2. Water irrigation
3. Toothpick (very carefully)

**Do NOT Use:**
- Sharp metal objects
- Excessive force
- Anything that could push object deeper

## Emergency Pain Management

### Over-the-Counter Medications
**For Adults:**
- Ibuprofen: 400-600mg every 6-8 hours (maximum 2400mg/day)
- Acetaminophen: 500-1000mg every 6-8 hours (maximum 3000mg/day)
- Can alternate ibuprofen and acetaminophen for severe pain

**For Children:**
- Follow age-appropriate dosing on package
- Consult pediatrician if unsure
- Avoid aspirin in children under 18

### Natural Pain Relief Methods
**Cold Therapy:**
- Ice pack on outside of cheek
- 20 minutes on, 20 minutes off
- Reduces swelling and numbs pain

**Salt Water Rinse:**
- 1/2 teaspoon salt in 8 oz warm water
- Gentle swishing, don't swallow
- Helps reduce bacteria and inflammation

**Clove Oil:**
- Natural numbing agent
- Apply small amount to cotton swab
- Dab on affected tooth
- Use sparingly - can be irritating

## Preventing Dental Emergencies

### Protective Measures
**Sports Activities:**
- Wear custom-fitted mouthguard
- Especially important for contact sports
- Replace mouthguard annually

**Daily Habits:**
- Don't use teeth as tools
- Avoid chewing ice, hard candies, popcorn kernels
- Don't bite non-food objects (pens, fingernails)
- Cut hard foods into smaller pieces

### Regular Dental Care
**Preventive Appointments:**
- Regular cleanings and checkups
- Address small problems before they become emergencies
- Professional assessment of oral health

**Home Care:**
- Brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste
- Daily flossing
- Use mouthwash
- Replace toothbrush every 3 months

## Emergency Dental Kit

### Essential Items to Keep at Home
1. **Contact Information:**
   - Emergency dental number
   - Regular dentist contact
   - Local hospital emergency room

2. **Medications:**
   - Ibuprofen and acetaminophen
   - Temporary dental cement
   - Clove oil

3. **Supplies:**
   - Gauze pads
   - Cold compress
   - Small container with lid
   - Dental wax
   - Salt
   - Dental floss

4. **Instructions:**
   - Emergency procedure guide
   - Medication dosing information

## When to Go to Hospital Emergency Room

### Dental Injuries Requiring ER Care
- Jaw fracture or dislocation
- Severe facial trauma
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Signs of serious infection with fever
- Loss of consciousness from dental trauma

### What ER Can and Cannot Do
**ER Can Provide:**
- Pain medication
- Antibiotics for infection
- Treatment for trauma and bleeding
- X-rays for jaw fractures

**ER Cannot Provide:**
- Definitive dental treatment
- Root canals or fillings
- Tooth reimplantation
- Complex dental procedures

**Follow-up dental care will still be necessary**

## Post-Emergency Care

### After Emergency Treatment
1. Follow all dentist instructions carefully
2. Take prescribed medications as directed
3. Apply ice as recommended
4. Eat soft foods
5. Avoid smoking and alcohol
6. Schedule follow-up appointment

### Signs of Complications
**Contact dentist if you experience:**
- Increasing pain after 2-3 days
- Persistent bleeding
- New or worsening swelling
- Fever
- Allergic reaction to medications
- Foul taste or odor

### Recovery Expectations
**Normal After Emergency Treatment:**
- Mild to moderate discomfort for 2-3 days
- Some swelling for 24-48 hours
- Slight bleeding for a few hours
- Sensitivity to temperature

**Not Normal - Contact Dentist:**
- Severe pain increasing after treatment
- Excessive bleeding
- Signs of infection
- Numbness lasting more than 4 hours

## Special Considerations

### Pregnant Patients
- Most dental emergencies can be safely treated
- Inform dentist of pregnancy
- Some medications may be limited
- X-rays with protective shielding are safe

### Patients with Medical Conditions
**Heart Conditions:**
- May need antibiotic premedication
- Inform dentist of all heart medications

**Diabetes:**
- Dental infections can affect blood sugar
- Monitor glucose levels closely
- Healing may take longer

**Blood Thinners:**
- Increased bleeding risk
- Don't stop medications without physician approval
- Inform dentist of all blood-thinning medications

### Children's Dental Emergencies
**Special Considerations:**
- Children may not accurately describe pain
- Look for behavioral changes
- Baby teeth that are knocked out are not reimplanted
- Permanent teeth need immediate attention

**Calming Techniques:**
- Stay calm yourself
- Explain procedures simply
- Offer comfort items
- Praise cooperation

Remember: While this guide provides helpful information for dental emergencies, it does not replace professional dental care. When in doubt, always contact a dental professional or seek emergency care.
